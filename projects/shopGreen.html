ShopGreen

A Google Chrome extension paired with a website that allows users to track their shopping habits and find more sustainable options for their closet by generating a sustainability score for online products and reporting their environmental impact to users.



Background

This project was developed in February of 2022 during the UT Austin Women in Computer Science Hackathon alongside two other developers, with a timeline of 24 hours.

The categories that our project aimed to target were “environmentalism” and “data visualization”. When pairing these two categories together, my partners and I hoped to illustrate the impact of harmful habits that many people are engaged in but may not be aware of. Our goal was to help cultivate meaningful impact and motivation to improve current conditions through changes that are easy to implement and understand. This led us to create an application that targets the fast fashion industry, due to its prevalence among our population.



My Role

I was the Lead UX Designer in this project; I generated the wireframe for the website version of our application and assisted with the wireframe for the Google Chrome extension of our application. I worked with my partners to create iterations of the wireframe while conducting research and studying strategies for our application to become effective. Through consistent collaboration with my partners, we identified the key features that should be included in our application. Finally, our group jointly created a slide deck to present our application in front of the judges in the hackathon.



Problem

A rising issue in our society is “fast fashion”, which is described as purchasing low-priced but stylish clothing to meet current trends. This harms the environment, as the pollution, waste, and emissions from fast fashion byproducts like plastic fibers fuel the triple planetary crisis. (https://unep.org/news-and-stories/story/environmental-costs-fast-fashion) At the same time, “the fashion industry consumes one-tenth of all of the water used industrially to run factories and clean products. Approximately 20% of the wastewater worldwide is attributed to this process, which accumulates over time. As many factories moved overseas as stated previously, they may be in countries without strict environmental regulations, resulting in untreated water to enter the oceans. Regrettably, the wastewater created is extremely toxic and in many cases, cannot be treated to become safe again… Additionally, due to the number of cut outs for the clothing, a large amount of materials are being wasted as they cannot be used any further for one certain type of production. 57% of all discarded clothing ends up in landfill; the landfills start to pile up, then the trash is moved to an area to be incinerated” (https://psci.princeton.edu/tips/2020/7/20/the-impact-of-fast-fashion-on-the-environment). It is estimated by the Ellen Macarthur Foundation that a truckload of abandoned textiles is dumped in landfill or incinerated every second.



Solution

To increase awareness of the previously mentioned harmful processes and consequences involved in fast fashion, our group created a product that supports the habits of shopping more sustainably and keeps people accountable for their actions. While we track people’s shopping habits, we generate a sustainability score that encapsulates the environmental impact of items that they peruse or add to their shopping carts. The sustainability score takes into account the amount of pollution, waste, and emissions involved in producing those items. This ultimately guides users to find more sustainable options by providing an easy method for tracking the degree of harm associated with purchasing such items.



Project Summary

Our product consists of a Google Chrome extension that allows users to track their shopping habits to find more sustainable options by generating a sustainability scores that reports the environmental impact of items to users. This extension also allows users to keep track of their budget when shopping so that they can remain conscious of their spending habits in addition to their impact on the environment.


Another feature of this extension includes a section dedicated to describing the materials that an item is composed of, breaking down the reasoning behind a given sustainability score. After reading through this information, users can choose to add this item to their cart and/or forward this information to their friends to help more people become aware of their environmental impact from shopping certain products.


This extension is designed to be accessible on every platform that users visit when shopping online, with the goal of guiding users to create a knowledgeable decision on what they can add to their wardrobe while respecting the environment that sustains us.


From this Google Chrome extension, users can be redirected to our application’s website, which further builds upon the information presented in the extension through the inclusion of visual diagrams that illustrate the composition of items in people’s shopping carts. A tracker page breaks down the composition of items in a user’s shopping cart by their material and includes several graphs to reveal the ratios.


Finally, users can sign in to their accounts to access more features like connecting with friends and sharing their shopping cart details with one another. This interactive experience continues to cultivate accountability, as their environmental impacts become more public, and it cultivates community, as users promote habits that protect the Earth together.

What I Learned

An important skill I learned from this project was how to make the prototype more interactive, especially since we had designed multiple drop-down features in the website version. For example, I started with the simple idea of creating two identical pages on our Figma wireframe and overlaying a drop-down box in one of the pages to imitate the effect of a drop-down box suddenly appearing on the screen after adding a connection from the first page (without the drop-down) to the second page (with the drop-down). After our team completed the basic outlines of our entire design, I was able to find a better way to implement this drop-down feature by exploring through Figma and searching through the internet. I eventually implemented a popup overlay modal that made the work much more clean and efficient.


Another important skill I learned was how to work in iterations. As a recovering perfectionist, I had to learn that the initial planning phases are never flawless and that there is always room for improvement. As a result, this project allowed me the opportunity to create several versions of wireframes and varying levels of wireframes to improve upon our working features and create a final product that could be easily understood. This was very encouraging, as our group was able to evolve and grow throughout the entire process.
