pomoplay

A mobile application that enables users to create better studying habits by blocking distractions from their phones with a lockdown feature and rewarding good study habits with virtual coins that can be used to play various games on the application.



Background

This project was developed in February of 2023 during the UT Austin Women in Computer Science Hackathon alongside one other developer, with a timeline of 10 hours.

The theme of this year's hackathon was "arcade", so my partner and I wanted to incorporate some form of play into our application. After spending time to ideate possible products, we determined to create a fun and effective way for students to study. Thus, our application aims to eliminate distractions for people while they are studying but also rewards them with breaks (e.g. playing a quick game) at short intervals after they have completed parts of their study session. This process was inspired by the Pomodoro Technique.



My Role

I was the Lead UX Designer in this project, and I created all of the wireframes for our application. I also conducted research and studied strategies for our application to become effective. I consistently collaborated with my partner to coordinate and discuss key features that should be included in our application. Finally, I created a slide deck that we used to present our application in front of the judges in the hackathon.



Problem

The elements of both "play" and "study" are important in the life of college students. However, it is important for people to find a balance between these two elements if they want to continue their education while remaining happy and sane. When researching the prevalence of scenarios where “play” and “study” are out of balance, we found studies yielding the following observations:


  • “The ability to strike a perceived sense of balance between work and life represents a challenge for many in academic and research sectors around the world… Spending time on self-care and relaxation is a necessity in life to maintain a healthy body and mind, leading to a fulfilling lifestyle. This, in turn, will enable [people] to achieve peak performance and productivity in the workspace” (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8282063/#pcbi.1009124.ref028). In other words, people should set aside time each day for an activity that they enjoy, which includes playing their favorite games for short intervals of time.
  • “Mentally demanding work causes strain reactions that increase in magnitude with the duration of work… [As a result,] students might require health-promoting scheduling of work and leisure to avoid an accumulation of strain”, i.e. burnout (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC10298359/).
  • A common reason why people tend to procrastinate is because of poor time management skills or because some tasks are too overwhelming. “In a 2013 study, Dr. Pychyl and Dr. Sirois found that procrastination can be understood as ‘the primacy of short-term mood repair… over the longer-term pursuit of intended actions.’ Put simply, procrastination is about being more focused on ‘the immediate urgency of managing negative moods’ than getting on with the task, Dr. Sirois said. The particular nature of our aversion depends on the given task or situation. It may be due to something inherently unpleasant about the task itself… but it might also result from deeper feelings related to the task, such as self-doubt, low self-esteem, anxiety or insecurity” (https://www.nytimes.com/2019/03/25/smarter-living/why-you-procrastinate-it-has-nothing-to-do-with-self-control.html).


    • Solution

    While many people have different study techniques, we found that the Pomodoro study technique was the most trending amongst the college-age population. Additionally, it was effective because of its ability to “alternate pomodoros — focused work sessions — with frequent short breaks to promote sustained concentration and stave off mental fatigue. The 25-minute work sprints are the core of the method [with 5-minute break sessions afterward]” (https://todoist.com/productivity-methods/pomodoro-technique). This technique is effective because it breaks down complex projects into manageable action items, making it less likely for people to procrastinate in the attempt to temporarily engage in short-term mood repair. This technique simultaneously offers a balance between work and life, as the break sessions offer relief from burnout.


    There is also an opportunity for self-improvement during this process, as the Pomodoro technique teaches more about consistency than perfection. Each session is a fresh start for users to reevaluate their goals, challenge themselves to focus, and limit distractions. This system can further be modified and adapted to each individual, which is helpful in the case where the 25-minute work/5-minute break does not work for someone. “In that case, they can find a time frame that does work. The idea is to break bigger tasks into smaller ones with uninterrupted focused work, followed by breaks to relax your mind. Regular breaks are important to do efficient work” (https://science.nichd.nih.gov/confluence/pages/viewpage.action?pageId=160956640).



    Project Summary

    This application enables users to create better studying habits. To implement the Pomodoro technique with a gaming component, we planned to create a mobile app that helps users focus on their tasks by implementing a lockdown page that prevents them from getting distracted by other apps on their phones.


    On this lockdown page, users first input the amount of time they want to study for and the number of breaks they would like to take throughout their study session. Once the timer starts for their study session, the app "locks" their phones such that they are unable to swipe out of the application until after the entire study session is completed. There is an emergency “stop” button if they must leave the app for emergencies, but they must enter a password and confirm their desire to stop studying to escape the lockdown feature.


    When it's time for a break, the app notifies users that they can step away from their study materials and offers them the opportunity to play a short game (approximately five minutes long) hosted on our app if they are interested. This cycle continues until the entire study session is over.


    To incentivize users to study more often and reward them for their good study habits, we also implemented a currency system in the app. For every five minutes that our users study, they receive a virtual coin that can be accumulated to purchase games (e.g. Solitaire, Minesweeper) in the games tab of our application. Users also will receive badges whenever they hit certain milestones on our app (e.g. continuously utilizing the app for 5 hours), which are displayed on a user's profile page.

    What I Learned

    Throughout this project, I learned more about creating organizational structures in my wireframe that are both aesthetically pleasing and easy to follow. For example, I spent more time considering the color palette and layout of our product so that the application would be calming for our users to use while they are studying. I also made sure to consider the contrast of elements against each other so that they are distinguishable and denote their significance (e.g. the “stop” button on the lockdown page was made red since it is universally recognized as the color for stopping).


    Additionally, I learned about the importance of grouping elements such as frames, text boxes, and assets together on Figma so that they can be efficiently identified and manipulated on each page. This was helpful in the long run, as it saved time when a small detail had to be changed that would later affect other elements. It was also helpful to understand which groups belonged to which page for coding purposes, as ReactJS heavily relies on components, and organizing these elements into components in the wireframe conveniently reduced the amount of thinking required for creating components when building the user interface.
